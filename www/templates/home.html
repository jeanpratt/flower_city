About

We’re driving south on Mount Hope Avenue away from the center of Rochester, N.Y. Within only a few minutes we pass the intersection of the Ford Street Bridge and our gray concrete surroundings transform into a forest of green. Trees are in abundance here and although this area looks more akin to the suburbs, we are still within the city limits.

Names like Azalea Road, Lilac Drive and Cypress Street do not evoke images of a bustling industrial boomtown in the mid-1800s. However, these street names are an accurate reminisces of the time period. While mills along the Genesee River were producing around 550,000 barrels of grain a new horticultural industry was taking bloom on the cities outskirts. Men like James Vick, Joseph Harris, Charles Crosman and the Brown Brothers built successful seed and nursery businesses in the area.

While all of these nurserymen would help to transform Rochester from the “Flour City” to the “Flower City,” it would be two immigrant nurserymen -- one from Germany, the other from Northern Ireland -- that would dominate the worldwide horticulture industry of the time and would spark the development of Rochester’s park system with a 20-acre donation of land.
